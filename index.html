Understanding Memory: Implicit & Explicit

Introduction

Memory is the cornerstone of our cognitive processes. It allows us to store, retain, and later retrieve information, enabling us to function daily. Understanding the types of memory is essential in various fields, from psychology to education.

Implicit Memory

Implicit memory, also known as non-declarative memory, operates without conscious awareness. It is responsible for skills and routines, such as riding a bicycle or typing on a keyboard. These memories are stored in the basal ganglia and are retrieved automatically without us actively thinking about them.

For example, when you drive a car, the procedural skills involved (steering, braking) are part of your implicit memory.

Explicit Memory

Explicit memory, also referred to as declarative memory, requires conscious effort to recall. This type of memory is divided into two categories: episodic memory (personal experiences) and semantic memory (facts and knowledge).

For instance, recalling the date of your friend's birthday or your last vacation are examples of explicit memory. These memories are primarily stored in the hippocampus and are consciously retrieved.

Question 1

Frank is trying to learn the names of all the students in his English course. Because Frank is actively and consciously remembering and recalling the names of students, this is considered ______ memory.

  • A. Implicit
  • B. Explicit
  • C. Episodic
  • D. Procedural

Answer: B. Explicit

Question 2

Katie is visiting Amsterdam, where riding bicycles is a common form of transportation. Even though she hasn’t ridden in a long time, Katie hops on and starts riding without actively thinking about what she is doing. Katie’s memory for riding a bicycle is an example of a(n) ______ memory.

  • A. Implicit
  • B. Sensory
  • C. Semantic
  • D. Explicit

Answer: A. Implicit
